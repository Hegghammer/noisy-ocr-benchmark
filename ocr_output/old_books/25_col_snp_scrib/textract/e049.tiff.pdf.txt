ESEARCH fails to show us that the ladies of France in their simple
Hersvingian and Carlovingian dresses paid any attention to the
formation of the waist or its display. But during the ninth century we
find the dresses worn extremely tight, and so made as to define the Vaist
and render it as shim as possible: and although the art of making the
description of corsets worn by the ladies of Rome W25 no doubt at that
time lost, the Tevived taste for slender figures led to the Deculiar form of
corsage known as cottes bardies, which were much stiffened and worn
extremely tight. These took the place of the quaint, oddly-formed
robes we see draping the figures of queens. The
